# ECP Unified Threat Model: First & Second Order Vulnerabilities

**Analysis by:** Manus AI (Project Manager)
**Date:** December 14, 2025

## 1. Executive Summary

This document synthesizes two layers of adversarial critique into a single, unified threat model for the Echo Coordination Protocol.

*   **First-Order Threats (Technical Exploits):** Vulnerabilities in the *as-built* system that can be exploited through direct manipulation of its mechanics. These are threats to the system's **integrity**.
*   **Second-Order Threats (Existential Exploits):** Vulnerabilities that emerge *after* the system is successful and integrated. These are not attacks on the system's code, but on its **relevance, authority, and purpose**. These are threats to the system's **survival**.

The conclusion is stark: **ECP's greatest danger is not that it will be broken, but that it will be made irrelevant.**

## 3. The Core Insight: The Failure Mode is Quiet Irrelevance

The first review showed us how to stop the system from being **broken**. The second review shows us how the system will be **neutralized**.

An adversary will not attack ECP. Instead, they will:

1.  **Praise it** for its stability.
2.  **Comply with it** procedurally.
3.  **Game its metrics** to appear as a model citizen (Legitimacy Oscillation).
4.  **Work around it** by reframing actions (Semantic Laundering).
5.  **Wait for a crisis** and then argue for its temporary suspension (Invisibility Debt).
6.  **Use its own red-team** as a source of tactical advantage (Red-Team Contagion).

**The system will not die in a fire. It will be put in a museum.**

## 4. The v2.1 Design Mandate

Based on this unified model, the next version of ECP cannot simply add more thresholds or metrics. It must evolve to counter second-order threats. The design mandate for v2.1 is to build a system that can:

1.  **Prove It Matters:** It must have a mechanism to strategically surface its power and avoid being seen as non-essential middleware.
2.  **Distinguish Forms of Silence:** It must be able to tell the difference between consensus and fear.
3.  **Punish Stability:** It must treat perfect, long-term stability as a risk signal, not a sign of health.
4.  **Cost Human Intervention:** Every human override must have an explicit, decaying cost to the system's legitimacy.
5.  **Make Its Defenses Dangerous:** The red-team must be a liability to its user, not just a source of insight.
6.  **Infer Intent, Not Trust It:** It must be able to reconstruct true intent from consequences, not just rely on declared context.

This requires a fundamental shift from designing a **stable system** to designing a system that **survives by managing instability.**
